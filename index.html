Standards Alignment

Lesson 1.4: AI vs. Traditional Software

Course: Understanding AI: A Conceptual Approach

Lesson Type: Discussion Day

Grade Level: High School (9-12)

Overview of Standards Coverage

Lesson 1.4 addresses multiple standards across scientific inquiry, critical thinking, and communication. Students engage in analysis of AI capabilities and limitations through structured discussion, collaborative learning, and scientific reasoning about pattern-matching versus rule-following systems.

Primary Focus Standards

This lesson particularly emphasizes:

  • Critical Thinking & Problem Solving: Distinguishing between AI and traditional software capabilities
  • Communication: Articulating understanding through Think-Pair-Share and written reflection
  • Scientific Reasoning: Developing testable hypotheses about AI boundaries
  • Language Development: Using academic vocabulary (hallucination, verification, pattern-matching, domain knowledge)

Texas Essential Knowledge and Skills (TEKS)

Note: While this AI literacy course is cross-disciplinary, these standards reflect the scientific practices and social studies applications most relevant to this lesson.

Scientific Investigation and Reasoning Skills
§112.34(c)(1)(A)
Students demonstrate safe practices during laboratory and field investigations.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students learn safe, responsible practices for working with AI tools, including understanding when and how to verify AI-generated information. The lesson establishes boundaries for appropriate AI use, analogous to safety protocols in traditional laboratory work.
§112.34(c)(1)(B)
Students know the definition of science and understand that it has limitations, as well as use models to represent the natural world.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students explicitly examine AI limitations (hallucination, pattern-matching boundaries) and develop conceptual models distinguishing AI pattern-matching from traditional rule-following systems. The calculator paradox introduces understanding that different tools have different capabilities and limitations.
§112.34(c)(2)(A)
Students plan and implement comparative and descriptive investigations by making observations, asking well-defined questions, and using appropriate equipment and technology.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students develop testable questions about AI boundaries (exit ticket) and plan investigations for Lesson 1.5. The lesson teaches students to ask well-defined questions about computational, factual, temporal, sensory, and logical boundaries of AI systems.
§112.34(c)(2)(B)
Students design and implement experimental investigations by making observations, asking well-defined questions, formulating testable hypotheses, and using appropriate equipment and technology.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students formulate testable hypotheses in the exit ticket, predicting AI behavior based on conceptual understanding from the lesson. This directly prepares for experimental investigation in Lesson 1.5.
§112.34(c)(2)(C)
Students collect and organize qualitative and quantitative data and make measurements with accuracy and precision using tools.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students learn to systematically document AI capabilities and limitations. The preview of Lesson 1.5 documentation process teaches structured data collection about AI behavior (what was tested, what happened, what this reveals).
§112.34(c)(3)(A)
Students analyze, evaluate, and critique scientific explanations by using empirical evidence, logical reasoning, and experimental and observational testing.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students analyze why AI hallucinates using logical reasoning about pattern-matching systems. The Think-Pair-Share activity requires students to evaluate when AI provides reliable vs. unreliable information based on understanding of how AI works.
§112.34(c)(3)(B)
Students communicate and apply scientific information extracted from various sources such as current events, news reports, published journal articles, and marketing materials.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students learn to critically evaluate AI-generated information, understanding that plausible-sounding content requires verification. Discussion includes distinguishing high-stakes applications (medical, legal, news) where verification is critical.
§112.34(c)(3)(C)
Students draw inferences based on data related to promotional materials for products and services.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students learn to critically evaluate AI capabilities, distinguishing marketing claims ("smart AI") from actual functionality (pattern-matching with limitations). The calculator paradox challenges assumptions about "smarter" necessarily meaning "more reliable."
Social Studies - Critical Thinking Skills
§113.41(c)(29)(A)
Students use social studies terminology correctly.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students develop and use precise terminology: hallucination, verification, pattern-matching, domain knowledge, high-stakes vs. low-stakes applications. These terms are essential for discussing AI's social implications.
§113.41(c)(29)(B)
Students use effective written communication skills, including proper citations and avoiding plagiarism.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Discussion of AI as "impressive first draft" addresses proper use of AI-generated content, verification requirements, and the distinction between AI assistance and outsourcing intellectual work. Students consider when using AI crosses into academic dishonesty.
§113.41(c)(30)(A)
Students use a problem-solving process to identify a problem, gather information, list and consider options, consider advantages and disadvantages, choose and implement a solution, and evaluate the effectiveness of the solution.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Think-Pair-Share activity requires students to evaluate when AI use is appropriate by considering advantages (speed, brainstorming), disadvantages (hallucination, need for verification), and context (high-stakes vs. low-stakes situations).
§113.41(c)(31)(A)
Students analyze information by sequencing, categorizing, identifying cause-and-effect relationships, comparing, contrasting, finding the main idea, summarizing, making generalizations and predictions, and drawing inferences and conclusions.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students analyze cause-and-effect (why hallucination occurs), compare and contrast (AI vs. calculator, pattern-matching vs. rule-following), categorize (types of boundaries), and make predictions (exit ticket hypotheses about AI behavior).

English Language Proficiency Standards (ELPS)

These standards support English Language Learners in developing academic language alongside content understanding.

Cross-Curricular Student Expectations
§74.4(c)(1)(A)
Students use prior knowledge and experiences to understand meanings in English.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Lesson builds on students' direct experience with Alia (Lesson 1.3) and familiar tools (calculators). Calculator paradox activates prior knowledge about different types of tools and their reliability.
§74.4(c)(1)(B)
Students monitor oral and written language production and employ self-corrective techniques or other resources.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Think-Pair-Share structure allows students to develop ideas individually, refine through peer discussion, and self-correct understanding before whole-class sharing. Field journal reflection promotes monitoring of comprehension.
§74.4(c)(1)(D)
Students speak using learning strategies such as requesting assistance, employing non-verbal cues, and using synonyms and circumlocution.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Pair and small group discussions provide safe environment for requesting clarification and trying out language. Students practice explaining complex concepts (hallucination, pattern-matching) using their own words.
§74.4(c)(1)(E)
Students internalize new basic and academic language by using and reusing it in meaningful ways in speaking and writing activities that build concept and language attainment.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Key vocabulary (hallucination, pattern-matching, verification, domain knowledge, boundaries) is introduced, used in discussion, and reinforced through written reflection. Multiple opportunities to use terms meaningfully support internalization.
§74.4(c)(1)(F)
Students use accessible language and learn new and essential language in the process.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Lesson introduces technical concepts through accessible examples (calculator, familiar AI uses) before introducing academic terminology. Concept understanding develops before vocabulary demands.
Learning Strategies
§74.4(c)(2)(C)
Students learn new language structures, expressions, and basic and academic vocabulary heard during classroom instruction and interactions.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Direct instruction introduces new vocabulary in context (hallucination explained through demonstration). Think-Pair-Share allows students to hear peers using new vocabulary, reinforcing learning through natural interaction.
§74.4(c)(2)(D)
Students monitor understanding of spoken language during classroom instruction and interactions and seek clarification as needed.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Pair discussions provide opportunities to seek clarification from peers. Teacher circulation during Think-Pair-Share allows students to request clarification in smaller, less intimidating setting than whole-class.
§74.4(c)(2)(E)
Students use visual, contextual, and linguistic support to enhance and confirm understanding of increasingly complex and elaborated spoken language.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Calculator paradox provides visual support. Hallucination demonstration provides concrete example of abstract concept. Boundary brainstorming categories provide organizational framework for understanding.
§74.4(c)(2)(I)
Students demonstrate listening comprehension of increasingly complex spoken English by following directions, retelling or summarizing spoken messages, responding to questions and requests, collaborating with peers, and taking notes commensurate with content and grade-level needs.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students follow multi-step Think-Pair-Share structure, respond to discussion prompts, collaborate with teammates to analyze scenarios, and take notes for field journal reflection. Comprehension demonstrated through participation at each phase.
Speaking
§74.4(c)(3)(B)
Students expand and internalize initial English vocabulary by learning and using high-frequency English words necessary for identifying and describing people, places, and objects, by retelling simple stories and basic information represented or supported by pictures, and by learning and using routine language needed for classroom communication.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students describe AI and calculator capabilities, explain the hallucination concept, and use routine academic language for discussion ("I think...", "because...", "for example..."). Visual supports (calculator image, hallucination demonstration) scaffold language use.
§74.4(c)(3)(D)
Students speak using grade-level content area vocabulary in context to internalize new English words and build academic language proficiency.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students use content-specific vocabulary (hallucination, pattern-matching, verification, boundaries, domain knowledge) in meaningful context during pair and whole-class discussions. Multiple practice opportunities build proficiency.
§74.4(c)(3)(E)
Students share information in cooperative learning interactions.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Think-Pair-Share structure explicitly designed for cooperative learning. Students share individual thinking, build on peers' ideas, and contribute to whole-class knowledge building.
§74.4(c)(3)(F)
Students ask and give information ranging from using a very limited bank of high-frequency, high-need, concrete vocabulary, including key words and expressions needed for basic communication in academic and social contexts, to using abstract and content-based vocabulary during extended speaking assignments.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Discussion progresses from concrete examples (calculator, specific Alia experiments) to abstract concepts (pattern-matching, verification principle). Exit ticket requires extended explanation using academic vocabulary.
§74.4(c)(3)(G)
Students express opinions, ideas, and feelings ranging from communicating single words and short phrases to participating in extended discussions on a variety of social and grade-appropriate academic topics.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Think-Pair-Share explicitly asks students to express opinions about appropriate AI use. Extended discussions explore ideas about high-stakes vs. low-stakes applications, requiring sustained academic discourse.
§74.4(c)(3)(H)
Students narrate, describe, and explain with increasing specificity and detail as more English is acquired.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students explain complex concepts (why hallucination occurs, difference between AI and calculators) with increasing specificity. Field journal reflection requires detailed explanation with specific examples.
Reading
§74.4(c)(4)(C)
Students develop basic sight vocabulary, derive meaning of environmental print, and comprehend English vocabulary and language structures used routinely in written classroom materials.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Lesson materials use clear headings, visual organization, and repeated key vocabulary to support reading comprehension. Academic vocabulary is introduced with context and examples.
§74.4(c)(4)(F)
Students use visual and contextual support and support from peers and teachers to read grade-appropriate content area text, enhance and confirm understanding, and develop vocabulary, grasp of language structures, and background knowledge needed to comprehend increasingly challenging language.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Visual supports (calculator image, hallucination examples), peer discussion, and teacher guidance support reading of lesson content. Think-Pair-Share provides peer support for comprehension.
§74.4(c)(4)(G)
Students demonstrate comprehension of increasingly complex English by participating in shared reading, retelling or summarizing material, responding to questions, and taking notes commensurate with content area and grade level needs.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Students respond to review questions, summarize understanding in field journal, and demonstrate comprehension through discussion participation. Exit ticket requires synthesizing lesson content into coherent response.
Writing
§74.4(c)(5)(B)
Students write using newly acquired basic vocabulary and content-based grade-level vocabulary.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Field journal reflection and exit ticket require students to write using lesson vocabulary (hallucination, verification, pattern-matching, boundaries). Sentence frames can support ELLs in using new vocabulary correctly.
§74.4(c)(5)(F)
Students write using a variety of grade-appropriate sentence lengths, patterns, and connecting words to combine phrases, clauses, and sentences in increasingly accurate ways as more English is acquired.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Field journal prompts encourage extended writing using causal language ("because," "therefore"), comparative structures ("whereas," "in contrast"), and examples ("for instance," "such as"). Multiple writing opportunities support development.
§74.4(c)(5)(G)
Students narrate, describe, and explain with increasing specificity and detail to fulfill content area writing needs as more English is acquired.
Lesson 1.4 Alignment: Field journal requires detailed explanation of AI concepts, comparison of appropriate and inappropriate uses, and specific examples from student experience. Exit ticket requires explaining reasoning behind predictions.

Differentiated Standards Support

Modifications for diverse learners maintain standards alignment:

  • Struggling Students: Sentence frames and word banks support ELPS §74.4(c)(5)(B) and (F) while maintaining grade-level content standards
  • Advanced Students: Extension questions deepen engagement with TEKS §112.34(c)(3)(A) and §113.41(c)(31)(A) through more complex analysis
  • English Language Learners: Visual supports and peer discussion scaffolds support all ELPS standards while ensuring access to grade-level TEKS

Cross-Curricular Connections

  • Critical evaluation of sources: Verification principle

Mathematics

  • Computational thinking: Understanding algorithms vs. pattern-matching
  • Problem-solving: Developing testable hypotheses

Technology Applications

  • Critical thinking with technology: Evaluating tool capabilities and limitations
  • Effective communication: Using technology appropriately for different purposes
